## Overview

Over one trillion dollars flows into religious and nonprofit organizations in the United States every year. An estimated ninety-two billion is lost to ecclesiastical crime — embezzlement, fraud, and financial misuse within religious institutions. The question I kept coming back to: who's watching? The answer is almost no one. The Prosperity Playbook is a provocative design artifact that makes this invisible regulatory architecture legible — not by critiquing it from the outside, but by selling it to you from the inside.

---

## Problem Space

The regulatory exemptions governing religious organizations in the U.S. were constructed piece by piece over more than a century. In 1894, churches were carved out of the first federal income tax. In 1954, 501(c)(3) status was codified. In 1969, Form 990 — the public financial disclosure every nonprofit must file — was created, and churches were exempted from it. In 1984, Congress passed the Church Audit Procedures Act, making it extraordinarily difficult for the IRS to audit a church.

The result is two structural shields. First, a five-hundred-million-dollar megachurch and a fifty-person community church have identical disclosure obligations: none. Second, even when the IRS suspects misconduct, the procedural bar is so high that audits often fail on technicalities before they begin. There's also voluntary self-regulation through the ECFA, but it's funded by the organizations it accredits — and it consistently acts only after journalists or lawsuits have already exposed problems.

The regulatory framework wasn't designed for a trillion-dollar sector. It was built for small mid-century community churches. The sector grew seven times over. The rulebook stayed the same.

## Research

I mapped the full system: how money flows from congregants to leadership, how leadership converts financial power into political lobbying that blocks enforcement, how the absence of enforcement prevents disclosure, and how without disclosure congregants can't evaluate how their money is used — so they keep giving. Every feedback loop that should create accountability has been structurally disabled — by law, by ideology, or by social pressure.

Prosperity theology sits alongside this architecture, providing ideological justification: giving is framed as an investment God will return, and leadership wealth is presented as proof the theology works. That's a closed, unfalsifiable loop. Whistleblowers face retaliation, ostracism, and spiritual manipulation — "touch not God's anointed" — so others stay silent.

The core finding: no single actor is the villain. The system is.

I also conducted case study research into specific organizations — including the LDS Church and Ensign Peak Advisors ($57 billion in U.S. equities across 13 separately registered SEC filing entities), Kenneth Copeland Ministries, Churchome, Elevation Church, and Second Baptist Houston — to ground the artifact in documented, verifiable practices.

## Design Process & Key Decisions

My design question: **How might we make the invisible regulatory advantages of religious organizations legible?** Not fix. Reveal.

The critical decision was the format. A report tells you a loophole exists. I wanted to make you feel what it's like to be inside the logic. That led to the Trojan Horse mechanism: an artifact that presents itself as a strategic financial framework for senior pastors — but whose real audience is everyone else.

The tone was deliberate. The Prosperity Playbook reads like a McKinsey report for churches. "Optimize your ministry's financial position." "Regulatory advantages already available to your organization." Professional consulting language applied to tax exemption. Every strategy described is legal. Every practice is documented. Every tactic is currently in use.

The other key decision was personal. I'm a believer. This project is not a critique of faith or the church. It's a critique of a regulatory system that fails the people inside it — including people like me. Naming that upfront was non-negotiable; it's the ethical frame for the entire project.

## The Artifact

The Prosperity Playbook is an interactive web experience. Users begin by building a ministry profile — organization size, structure, revenue — which the system uses to tailor which strategies apply. Five modules each map to a specific regulatory advantage, and each highlights a real organization currently using that strategy:

The Legal Foundations module walks through the Association of Churches structure — a multi-entity umbrella that eliminates disclosure obligations entirely. Its case study is the LDS Church, which used this architecture to build a $57 billion investment portfolio. When the SEC discovered it, the settlement was $5 million — 0.01% of the portfolio.

Each module includes implementation steps, legal references, and communication frameworks for handling questions from congregants. Testimonials are woven throughout, framed as success stories: "When a board member raised questions about how benevolence funds were allocated, I had language I didn't have before. The issue simply moved on."

The reveal comes when the user steps back and realizes: everything they just read is real. The discomfort is the critique working.

## Reflection

The sharpest insight from this project isn't about religion. It's about what design can do that other forms struggle to. Journalism has done real work exposing individual cases of financial misconduct in religious organizations. But cases frame the problem as a story of bad actors. The system is the story. The Playbook's move is to sell you that system in its own voice — a report tells you a loophole exists, the Playbook makes you feel what it's like to be inside the logic.

The provocation isn't "churches are bad." It's "the system was designed this way, and here's the user manual to prove it."

Religious liberty is a foundational right. But the regulatory exemptions built in its name have created the least financially accountable institutions in American public life. That paradox is what I kept pulling at — and what The Prosperity Playbook makes impossible to look away from.
