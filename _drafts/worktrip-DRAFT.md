## Overview

As a Project Administrator at Barge Design Solutions (an architecture and engineering firm), I had a front-row seat to how broken the company's travel process was. Trip requests were paper-based. Mileage tracking was manual and inconsistent. Employees had no visibility into whether their request was submitted, in review, or booked. Admins drowned in email chains and paper reports. I identified the problem, pitched the solution, and designed WorkTrip — an internal app that streamlines travel requests, gives employees real-time visibility, and provides admins with cleaner, more efficient data.

---

## Design Goals

1. **Simplify logistics** by centralizing trip requests and updates in one place
2. **Enhance clarity** with real-time visibility into itineraries and reports
3. **Reduce admin burden** through smart defaults and automated workflows

---

## Research

I conducted interviews and observed the existing workflow to understand where the process broke down. Three pain points dominated:

**Unclear Booking Status and Communication Gaps.** Employees struggled to track the status of travel requests — whether submitted, in review, or booked — due to fragmented communication and no centralized view. People submitted requests and then waited in the dark.

**Frustration and Inconsistency with Mileage Tracking.** Mileage reporting was manual, inconsistent, and prone to errors — leading to delays and distrust in reimbursements. Employees wanted a seamless, built-in way to track mileage on the go rather than reconstructing trips after the fact.

**Manual Processes Slow Admin Efficiency.** Admins relied heavily on paper-based reports and email chains to manage travel logistics, leading to lost time, missing data, and inconsistent records. Without centralized digital tracking, pulling reports or verifying bookings was tedious and error-prone.

---

## Design Process — Two Iterations

### Version 1

The first version focused on getting the core workflow right: a home page with quick access to key actions, easy access to company car information, and a summary page to review submitted data. This version validated the basic flow — employees could submit and track requests digitally for the first time.

### Version 2

Based on feedback from v1, the second iteration added:

**Progress Tracking.** A progress line showing where users are in the submission process — providing the real-time visibility that was the #1 pain point.

**Home Page as Info Hub.** The home page became a centralized dashboard rather than just a launch point — surfacing active trips, pending requests, and upcoming travel at a glance.

**Vehicle Inspection.** A safety initiative that needed to be integrated — pre-trip vehicle inspection checklists built directly into the app flow, ensuring compliance without adding friction.

## Validation

**Submission Without Struggle.** All testers successfully submitted a report without needing help, showing that the core flow is intuitive and user-friendly. The digital process was immediately faster and less error-prone than paper.

**Status Tracker = Instant Reassurance.** The status tracker stood out as the favorite feature — users felt more in control knowing exactly where their request was in the process. The anxiety of "did my request go through?" disappeared.

**Group Travel Coordination.** Testers frequently mentioned the need for easier group coordination tools, pointing to an opportunity for collaborative booking features in a future version.

---

## Reflection

WorkTrip stayed as a prototype — leadership was excited about it, but internal politics made implementation difficult. That tension taught me something important: solving the design problem is only half the work. Navigating organizational dynamics to get solutions adopted is its own design challenge. The vehicle inspection requirement added unexpected depth to the project, pushing me to think about compliance as a design constraint rather than an afterthought.

This project also shows something I value: I don't wait for a brief. I identified a broken process in my own workplace and designed the fix. That instinct — seeing systems that could work better and doing something about it — is what drew me to interaction design.
